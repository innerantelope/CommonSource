Governance.

CommonSource is structured as a data cooperative. This page is the public record of how it works.

Published by Community Media Alliance · In effect from founding · Questions: abeer@smartngo.org

Six Principles

What this cooperative stands on.

These are not aspirations. They are the structural commitments that govern how CommonSource operates, and they cannot be changed without a supermajority of member publishers.

1
Publishers own the corpus.

No entity â€” including Community Media Alliance â€” can sell, licence, or redirect the CommonSource corpus without the collective consent of member publishers. CMA builds and maintains the platform. It does not own what members bring to it.

2
Revenue flows back.

All revenue above operating costs is distributed to contributing publishers. Distribution is proportional to how their content is cited â€” not by traffic, not by virality, not by language, not by the size of the publication. The smallest community radio station earns the same rate per citation as the largest member.

3
The council rotates.

The CommonSource Council is geographically distributed, term-limited, and elected. No single country or organisation may hold more than 20% of seats. CMA holds no permanent seat. Power is redistributed by design, not rhetoric.

4
Decisions are published.

All council decisions, content standard changes, and licensing negotiations are published within 30 days of being made. There are no private governance decisions. The record is open.

5
Appeals are open.

Any publisher may appeal a decision to remove or demote their content. The appeals process is documented, binding, and free. No publisher can be removed from the index without a recorded decision and an opportunity to respond.

6
The corpus is not for sale.

A data licensing deal â€” including to any AI company â€” requires approval by a supermajority of 75% of member publishers, with full public disclosure of terms before the vote is held. No exceptions. No fast-tracking. No emergency clauses.

Content Eligibility

What belongs in the index.

CommonSource is not a general-purpose news archive. It is a specialist corpus of community and local media from the Global South. These criteria are precise by design.

Eligible
✓Community radio stations with original broadcasts or transcripts
✓Local digital and print newsrooms serving a defined geographic area
✓Civil society organisation field documentation and programme reports
✓Community-produced journalism with named editorial responsibility
✓Development programme reports with original fieldwork
✓Public interest broadcasting organisations serving underserved areas
Not eligible

Editorial Standards

What CommonSource commits to â€” and what it does not.

These are the standards that govern what is indexed and how it is presented. CommonSource is a search engine, not a publisher â€” it does not produce journalism, but it holds the journalism it surfaces to clear requirements.

Attribution

Every piece of content in the index must have a named source, a publication name, and a date. Anonymous content, undated content, and unattributed aggregation are not eligible. Bylines are preserved in all search results.

Accuracy

CommonSource does not editorially verify the accuracy of indexed content â€” this responsibility rests with member publishers. However, the council reserves the right to remove content flagged as demonstrably false by a majority vote, with the decision published and open to appeal.

Language neutrality

No language is privileged in ranking or indexing. A Tamil-language community broadcast is indexed and surfaced on the same basis as an English-language report. Multilingual semantic embeddings ensure cross-language discovery without translation bias.

No ranking by engagement

Content is ranked by semantic relevance to the query, proximity to the subject, and source type â€” never by page views, social shares, or engagement signals. Virality has no weight in CommonSource search.

Appeals Process

Every publisher has the right to be heard.

Any publisher may appeal a decision to remove or demote their content. The process is simple, documented, and binding on the council. There is no fee. There is no bureaucratic barrier.

1
Submit a written appeal

The publisher submits a written appeal by email to the appeals address, stating the decision being appealed and the grounds. Appeals must be submitted within 30 days of the original decision.

Deadline: 30 days from decision
2
Council acknowledges receipt

The council acknowledges the appeal in writing within 5 working days, confirming the review panel composition. If any panel member has a conflict of interest, they are replaced before acknowledgement.

Timeline: 5 working days
3
Review by a panel of three

A panel of 3 council members â€” none of whom have a conflict of interest in the case â€” reviews the appeal. The publisher may submit additional documentation. The review is completed within 20 days of acknowledgement.

Timeline: 20 days from acknowledgement
4
Decision published with reasoning

The panel decision is communicated to the publisher and published in the public record within 7 days of the decision, with full reasoning. Decisions to uphold removals are subject to the same transparency standards as the original decision.

Published within 7 days
5
Further appeal to the full council

For decisions the publisher considers significant â€” removal of a substantial body of work, or decisions with editorial implications beyond their own content â€” a further appeal to the full council is available. The full council's decision is final.
